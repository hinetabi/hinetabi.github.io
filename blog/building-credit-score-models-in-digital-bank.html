Building Credit Score Models
in a Digital Bank

Le Thanh Binh  ·  2026

Shipping a credit score model is not just about training accuracy. It requires translating a regulatory-constrained business problem into a well-defined ML task, auditing messy banking data, running rigorous experiments, estimating business impact in terms stakeholders actually care about, and keeping the model healthy long after deployment.

This series walks through the full ML lifecycle scoped to one concrete use case: building a credit scoring model at a digital bank. Each post covers one stage in depth. The stages follow each other, but the lifecycle is a loop — monitoring feeds back into problem formulation, and every retrain makes the next one faster.

Why Credit Scoring?

Credit scoring is one of the most consequential ML applications in financial services — and one of the most technically demanding. It sits at the intersection of four challenges that test every stage of the lifecycle:

Regulated

Certain features are prohibited. Models must be explainable to regulators and model governance committees. Fairness across segments is not optional.

Censored data

The bank only observes outcomes for approved borrowers. Every model operates on a biased sample — those it previously decided to approve.

Asymmetric cost

A false negative (approving a defaulter) costs principal + collection. A false positive costs foregone margin. The optimal threshold is a business decision, not a statistical one.

Concept drift

Borrower behavior shifts with economic cycles. A model trained before a recession will be miscalibrated after it. Monitoring here is not operational hygiene — it is a risk management function.
