Multivariate Analysis — Where Credit Policy Lives

Le Thanh Binh · Data Scientist, Cake by VPBank · April 2026

EDAInteractionsCredit Policy

Section 01What Is It & Why Do We Need It?

What is multivariate analysis?

Multivariate analysis examines three or more variables simultaneously — how combinations of features interact, whether the effect of one feature depends on the value of another, and which feature combinations produce the most extreme risk profiles.

Why does it matter more in banking than in other domains?

Banking risk is inherently combinatorial. A single variable almost never tells the full story. Consider:

A Grade C loan sounds moderate risk. But a Grade C loan with no collateral, from a self-employed borrower with a DTI of 0.6, is a fundamentally different product from a Grade C loan backed by property from a salaried borrower. Bivariate analysis sees the grade. Only multivariate analysis sees the combination.

This is not just an academic observation. The most important credit policy rules in any lending institution are multivariate: "No loans above 500M VND to Grade D or below with no collateral." "Freelance borrowers require at least Grade B." These rules did not come from intuition — they came from exactly this kind of analysis applied to historical default data.

Why bivariate alone is not enough: employment_type = Freelance has a 12.4% average default rate. loan_grade = D has a 14.2% default rate. But Freelance + Grade D + no collateral has a 32% default rate — much higher than either factor alone would predict by simple addition. This superadditive interaction is invisible until you look at all three together.

Section 02What Is an Interaction Effect?

An interaction effect occurs when the impact of one feature on default risk changes depending on the value of another feature. Formally: the effect of A on default is not constant — it depends on B.

Interaction Effect — DTI Impact Depends on Collateral
DTI's effect on default is weaker when collateral exists — collateral modifies the risk of high DTI

The two lines diverge significantly as DTI increases. For borrowers with collateral (green line), even a high DTI of 0.7 results in only about an 8% default rate — the collateral provides security. For borrowers without collateral (red line), the same DTI pushes default rate above 30%. DTI and collateral are not independent; they interact. A model that ignores this interaction will systematically underestimate risk for high-DTI, uncollateralized borrowers.

Section 03Loan Grade × Collateral Type — Risk Grid

What is it?

A two-dimensional heatmap where rows are loan grades and columns are collateral types, with each cell showing the default rate for that exact combination. This is the most direct way to visualize a two-way interaction between categorical features.

Why does it reveal what bivariate analysis misses?

Bivariate analysis tells you Grade C has 7.8% default and "no collateral" has 15.8% default. Multivariate tells you Grade C with no collateral has 10.2% default — which is similar to Grade D with property collateral (9.8%). This equivalence is invisible from the two individual charts but immediately visible in the grid below.

Default Rate Grid: Loan Grade × Collateral Type
Each cell = observed default rate for that combination · Deeper red = higher risk

The Grade C ≈ Grade D insight: Grade C (No Collateral) = 10.2% default rate is nearly identical to Grade D (Property) = 9.8%. This means the bank's grade system and collateral requirement are not independent risk factors — they substitute for each other at the margin. A Grade C unsecured loan should be priced like a Grade D secured loan. This is an EDA finding that directly feeds into pricing and underwriting policy.

Section 04Faceted Analysis — Age Distribution by Segment

What is it?

A faceted chart creates a grid of small sub-charts — each showing the same distribution for a different subset of the data. It is the multivariate equivalent of a histogram: instead of one distribution for the whole population, you see how the distribution shifts across combinations of segments.

Why does it matter?

Facets reveal whether a pattern you see in the whole population is consistent across segments, or driven by one specific sub-group. If the age distribution of defaulters looks similar across all loan purposes, age is a genuine cross-cutting risk factor. If the pattern only holds for Business loans but not for Personal loans, the relationship is segment-specific — and a model that pools the populations together will miss this nuance.

Section 05From EDA to Credit Policy Rules

What is it?

Credit policy rules are hard cutoffs that the bank applies before a model score — "no loans to Grade F+ customers regardless of score," or "Freelance borrowers require collateral." These rules exist because in certain combinations, risk is so high that a model score offers no additional discrimination.

Why does multivariate EDA generate them?

A bivariate default rate of 14.2% for Grade D is concerning but manageable with higher pricing. A multivariate finding that Grade D + Freelance + no collateral produces a 32% default rate is not a pricing problem — it is a policy problem. No interest rate can be set high enough to make that combination viable in a regulated consumer lending context without becoming predatory.

Policy Rules Derived from Multivariate EDA
Each rule corresponds to a specific combination where default rate exceeds the viable threshold
Combination Found in EDAObserved Default RatePolicy Decision
Grade E–G + No Collateral38.1%Automatic decline regardless of score
Grade D + Freelance + No Collateral~32%Decline or require 2× security deposit
Grade C + No Collateral + DTI > 0.5~18%Require manual underwriter review
Grade A/B + Property Collateral<2%Eligible for fast-track automated approval

The data scientist's responsibility: When multivariate EDA reveals that a certain combination produces very high default rates, documenting and surfacing that finding is essential. The decision about whether to create a hard policy rule — vs. letting the model score handle it — belongs to the risk and business teams. But the data scientist must make the finding visible and quantifiable. An EDA report that only shows marginal distributions will miss these combinations entirely.
