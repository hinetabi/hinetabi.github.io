You've explored your data from every angle: structure, missingness, univariate distributions, bivariate relationships, multivariate interactions, outliers, and the target variable itself. Now comes the part that distinguishes a data scientist from a data analyst — translating all those observations into a coherent narrative that drives decisions.

EDA is not a phase that ends. It's a mindset that continues through feature engineering, model training, and even post-deployment monitoring. This final post captures the key insights from the full EDA journey and gives you the frameworks to document, communicate, and act on them.

Every significant EDA finding should map to a concrete downstream action. If an observation doesn't change something — a feature engineering decision, a modeling choice, a policy rule, a data quality fix — it wasn't worth documenting. 03 — PitfallsThe Most Common EDA Mistakes in Banking

After running EDA across many credit datasets, certain mistakes appear again and again. Most are not about missing a chart — they're about missing a question.

Top EDA Mistakes — and What They Cost
From model audits and post-deployment failures in consumer credit
MistakeWhat Goes WrongHow to Avoid
Treating ordinal as numeric loan_grade encoded as 1–7 implies Grade G is "7× more risky than A," which is not the relationship. Linear models are distorted. Use WoE encoding or target encoding for ordinal features in linear models; tree models handle ordinals natively
Ignoring missingness patterns Imputing credit_score with the population median erases the thin-file signal entirely — the missing rows ARE the high-risk population Always classify missingness as MCAR/MAR/MNAR first; create indicator flags before any imputation
Using accuracy on imbalanced targets A model predicting all non-defaults achieves 88% accuracy. Stakeholders approve it. The bank loses money on 100% of defaults. Always report AUC-ROC, KS statistic, and precision-recall curves. Never report accuracy alone.
Including immature vintages Training data includes Q4 2023 loans at 3% default rate (only 6 months old). Model is systematically over-optimistic. Post-deployment KPIs miss targets. Apply minimum 12-month maturity cutoff before defining the training population
Treating EDA as a one-time step Data changes over time. The distribution shift in new applicants renders your EDA conclusions stale. Model performance degrades silently. Build monitoring dashboards that re-run key EDA metrics on new incoming data monthly
Confusing correlation with causation high correlation between loan purpose "vacation" and default doesn't mean vacations cause defaults — it's an income/segment proxy. Wrong features get prioritized. For every correlation finding, ask: what's the actual causal mechanism? Does this make business sense?

04 — DocumentationWhat Goes in the Model Development Document

In Vietnamese banking, the Model Development Document (MDD) is the primary artifact that model validators, auditors, and the SBV will review. The EDA section of the MDD is not optional and must be comprehensive. Here is what examiners expect.

MDD EDA Section — Minimum Requirements
Based on SBV Circular 11 and Basel Committee model risk guidelines

On Information Value (IV): IV measures how well a feature separates defaulters from non-defaulters using WoE bins. IV < 0.02 = useless; 0.02–0.1 = weak; 0.1–0.3 = medium; >0.3 = strong. The IV ranking from your EDA directly dictates the feature shortlist for your scorecard — it's not a nice-to-have, it's a regulatory expectation.

05 — ApproachThe Hypothesis-Driven EDA Mindset

Undirected EDA — plotting everything and seeing what sticks — is slow and often misleading. The most effective credit risk analysts approach EDA as a series of falsifiable hypotheses, driven by business knowledge.

Before opening a dataset, write down your priors. In loan default modeling, standard hypotheses include: borrowers with higher DTI default more; salaried income is more stable than self-employed; longer loan terms increase default exposure; collateral reduces default severity even if it doesn't reduce probability. Then let the data confirm or contradict each one.

Hypothesis-Driven EDA — Banking Examples
Form hypotheses first; let data confirm or challenge them
HypothesisWhat EDA ShowsOutcome
"Higher DTI → higher default rate" DTI quartile analysis: Q1 (DTI <0.2) defaults at 4.1%, Q4 (DTI >0.6) at 27.3% ✓ Confirmed — DTI is a strong monotonic predictor
"Freelance income is unstable → more defaults" Freelance default rate 22.6% vs salaried 7.4% ✓ Confirmed — but also reflects lower income and higher DTI on average
"Loan grade already captures all credit risk" Within Grade C, no-collateral borrowers default at 10.2% vs 5.8% with property ✗ Rejected — collateral adds signal beyond grade; both should be in the model
"Larger loan amounts are riskier" Correlation: loan_amount ↔ default = +0.12 (weak); controlling for grade removes the effect ✗ Partially rejected — amount is a proxy for grade; grade dominates when both included
"Recent borrowers are less risky (better screening)" Vintage analysis: mature cohorts show 13–15% default; recent cohorts too immature to compare ? Inconclusive — need 18 months of data to validate; exclude from training set

When a hypothesis is rejected, that's not a failure — it's a finding. The fact that loan amount doesn't independently predict default (once grade is controlled for) is important information for feature selection and for communicating to stakeholders why a "bigger loan = more risk" intuition doesn't hold in the data.

06 — PhilosophyThe Right Mindset: EDA as Ongoing Dialogue

The best framing for EDA is not a checklist but a dialogue — between you, the data, and the business. Every chart answers one question and raises two more. Every anomaly is an invitation to understand the data-generating process better.

The hardest insight to act on: Sometimes EDA reveals that your data is fundamentally unsuitable for the model you intended to build. Maybe the observation window is too short. Maybe a key feature is missing for 60% of the population. Maybe the target definition changed mid-dataset. These discoveries feel like failure, but they are the most important outputs EDA can produce — they prevent you from building and deploying a model that will underperform in the real world.

In banking, where model errors translate directly into financial loss, regulatory scrutiny, or unfair outcomes for borrowers, EDA is not bureaucratic overhead. It is the intellectual foundation of everything that follows. A model built on unexamined data is a liability.

EDA never really ends: After deployment, monitoring dashboards should track the same distributions you explored here — DTI distribution, missing rate on credit_score, default rate by grade. When those distributions shift, your EDA findings may no longer apply. The model needs recalibration. EDA is not a phase — it's a practice.
