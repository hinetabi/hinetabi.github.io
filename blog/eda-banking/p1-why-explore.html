EDA for Banking Loans · Part 1 of 8

Why EDA Matters & Initial Data Exploration

Le Thanh Binh · Data Scientist, Cake by VPBank · April 2026

EDACredit RiskBanking

"If you torture the data long enough, it will confess to anything." — Ronald Coase. EDA is the discipline of not torturing the data — of understanding it honestly before making any demands of it.

Most data science courses teach EDA as a sequence of plot types: histogram, boxplot, heatmap, done. But in banking, where a scoring model directly determines who gets credit and at what price, that mechanical approach is not enough. EDA in banking is an act of due diligence — you are taking responsibility for the data quality, the feature logic, and the analytical assumptions before they are baked into a production system.

This series walks through every EDA stage using a realistic loan default dataset. Each part focuses on what the technique is and why it matters specifically in banking — not on code syntax.

Section 01Why EDA Is Non-Negotiable in Banking

What is EDA?

Exploratory Data Analysis is the process of systematically investigating a dataset to understand its structure, quality, distributions, and relationships — before building any model. It is the difference between knowing your data and assuming you know your data.

Why is it especially critical in banking?

In most domains, a model that performs slightly worse than expected is an inconvenience. In banking, it is a financial and regulatory event. The stakes create three non-negotiable reasons to do thorough EDA:

1. Data arrives broken. Loan data is assembled from multiple upstream systems — an origination platform, a core banking system, a credit bureau feed, an internal CRM — each with its own schemas, encodings, and quality levels. Joins between these systems routinely produce duplicated rows, mismatched types, and columns that look numeric but contain encoded sentinels like -999 or 0 representing "not available." EDA is the only way to catch these before they corrupt model training.

2. Wrong assumptions cost money. A model trained without EDA may discover that annual_income is right-skewed but use it as-is — causing tree depth to blow up on income outliers and degrading performance on the bulk of the population. Or it may treat a missing credit_score as zero, systematically mis-scoring thin-file customers who are actually a distinct risk segment.

3. Regulators require it. The State Bank of Vietnam (SBV), the Basel Committee, and internal model risk management frameworks all require that model development documents (MDDs) contain evidence of data quality validation, feature distribution analysis, and missing data treatment decisions. EDA is not just good practice — it produces the artifacts that let you deploy the model legally.

The silent failure problem: Data quality failures in banking are silent. A dtype mismatch does not throw an error — it silently converts a new category to NaN. A duplicate loan record does not announce itself — it inflates the weight of one customer in training. A -999 sentinel in an income column looks like a real (negative) value to every statistical function you run on it. These failures compound downstream into WoE tables, into feature importances, into production scores.

Section 02The 7 Questions EDA Answers

Before running a single line of analysis, write down what you expect the data to tell you. Then use EDA to test each expectation. Here are the seven questions a thorough EDA must answer for a loan default dataset:

Q1
What exactly do I have? — How many loan records, how many features, what are the data types, do they match the data dictionary? Did the join produce unexpected columns or duplicate rows?
Q2
What is missing, and why? — Which features have null values? Is the missingness random or systematic? Does absence encode information (e.g., no collateral offered, no credit history)?
Q3
How is each feature distributed? — Is annual_income right-skewed? Is credit_score bimodal? Are there impossible values like negative ages or interest rates above 200%?
Q4
How do features relate to each other? — Is credit_score correlated with num_late_payments as expected? Are any two features so correlated they are effectively duplicates?
Q5
What combination of features predicts default? — Does default rate increase when both loan grade is low and no collateral is present? Do interaction effects reveal policy rules the business hasn't articulated yet?
Q6
What are the outliers, and are they real? — Does a loan_amount of 5 billion VND represent a high-net-worth customer or a data entry error? Does num_late_payments = 50 represent a deeply delinquent borrower or a system bug?
Q7
Is the target variable well-defined and trustworthy? — What exactly is "default"? Is there class imbalance? Does default rate vary suspiciously by time period (suggesting data vintage issues)?

Section 04Initial Data Exploration

What is it?

The first physical contact with the dataset — verifying that what was received matches what was described, identifying the shape, checking that data types are correctly interpreted, and confirming there are no immediately obvious structural problems.

Why do we need it?

You cannot analyze data you do not understand. Initial exploration answers the most basic questions: how many rows, how many columns, what does a typical row look like, are there duplicate records? These seem trivial, but finding a 50,000-row dataset when you expected 200,000 — or finding the same loan_id appearing three times — surfaces problems that invalidate every subsequent analysis if left unchecked.

In banking specifically, this step also confirms that the dataset represents the correct population and time period. A dataset labeled "all consumer loans 2022–2024" that actually only contains approved loans from 2022 is a different dataset — one with selection bias baked in — and every distribution you analyze will be wrong.

Section 05Common Traps in Banking Data

What are they?

Patterns that look correct at a glance but hide serious problems. These traps are especially common in banking because data is assembled from legacy systems with long-standing conventions that were never designed for machine learning.

Banking Data Traps — Initial Inspection Checklist
Each trap has passed basic validation but will cause silent errors downstream
TrapWhat it looks likeWhy it's dangerousFix
Ordinal as integerloan_grade stored as 1–7Model assumes equal spacing between grades — Grade B is not exactly halfway between A and C in riskCast to ordered category
ID as featureloan_id, customer_id included in trainingModel memorizes IDs, producing perfect train accuracy and random test accuracyDrop all ID columns before training
Sentinel as valuecredit_score = -999 or income = 0Statistical functions treat as real values, pulling mean and variance entirely offReplace with NaN, then handle as missing
Leakage columndays_since_default, collection_statusFeature only has a value after the loan defaults — using it causes the model to cheatVerify each feature is available at the prediction point in time
Nominal as numericregion encoded as 1–63 (province codes)Model assumes province 63 is "more" than province 1 — a meaningless numeric relationshipCast to string category or use target encoding properly
Informative "None"collateral_type = NoneIf treated as missing and imputed, the signal is destroyed — "no collateral offered" is the actual informationKeep "None" as an explicit category

The loan_grade encoding trap in detail: Imagine your grading system assigns A=1, B=2, C=3, D=4, E=5, F=6, G=7. A linear model would then learn a coefficient for "grade" and apply it linearly — implying the risk difference between A and B equals the risk difference between F and G. In practice, the jump from F to G often carries far more incremental risk than the jump from A to B. The encoding destroys this non-linearity before training even begins.
