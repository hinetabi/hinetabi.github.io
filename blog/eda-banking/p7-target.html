Target Variable Deep Dive —
Understanding What You're Predicting

Le Thanh Binh · Data Scientist, Cake by VPBank · April 2026
Class Imbalance Target Definition Vintage Analysis

Before building any model, you must deeply understand the thing you're predicting. In credit risk, this is is_default — a binary label that appears simple but hides a minefield of definitional choices, temporal traps, and measurement ambiguities. How you define, explore, and validate this variable often matters more than the algorithm you choose.

Most EDA guides spend five minutes on the target variable: count the classes, check the ratio, move on. This part argues that you should spend proportionally more time on the target than on any other single feature — because every downstream model quality metric flows from it.

01 — DefinitionWhat Does "Default" Actually Mean?

In regulatory terms, the Basel Committee defines default as a borrower being 90+ days past due (DPD) on any material obligation. But in practice, different banks use different cutoffs, and the EDA analyst must verify which definition was applied before trusting any label.

Common is_default Definitions in Vietnamese Consumer Banking
Each definition captures a different risk horizon and population

SBV Group 3–5 SBV Circular 11, debt group ≥ 3 ~10–15% Regulatory reporting, Vietnam-specific

The observation window trap: A loan issued in Q4 2023 may not have had enough time to default by Q2 2024. If you include these "immature" loans in your training data, they'll appear as non-defaults even though some would have defaulted given more time. This right-censoring problem is why target variable EDA must always include a temporal component.

02 — ImbalanceClass Distribution and Why It Changes Everything

In most consumer loan portfolios, defaults are the minority class — typically 5–20%. This is not a coincidence: banks only survive by approving mostly good loans. But this structural imbalance creates serious problems for naive model training.

is_default — Class Distribution
Illustrative: 88% non-default vs 12% default in loan portfolio

A 7:1 imbalance means a model that predicts "never default" for every borrower achieves 88% accuracy — while being completely useless. Accuracy is the wrong metric. This is why EDA on the target variable must surface the imbalance explicitly, before anyone proposes using accuracy as a model evaluation criterion.

The accuracy illusion: Presenting a credit risk model with 88% accuracy to a bank stakeholder who doesn't understand imbalance is dangerous. They may approve a model that approves every application — causing massive losses. Always report precision, recall, F1, and AUC-ROC for imbalanced classification problems. Never use accuracy alone.

03 — SegmentationDefault Rate Across Key Segments

The overall 12% default rate is an aggregate that hides enormous variation by segment. EDA should compute and visualize the default rate across each major feature to understand where risk concentrates.

Default Rate by Segment — Selected Features
The overall rate of 12% masks extreme variation across groups

This segmentation view reveals that the headline 12% default rate is dragged up by Grade G (42.8%) and freelance borrowers (22.6%). Grade A salaried borrowers with property collateral likely default at under 3%. Building a single model to serve both populations may be the wrong architecture entirely — the EDA is pointing toward a segmented credit policy.

04 — TimeVintage Analysis — Default as a Time-Evolving Curve

A "vintage" is a cohort of loans originated in the same time period (month or quarter). Vintage analysis tracks how the cumulative default rate of each cohort evolves over the loan's life. This is critical for understanding whether your target label is meaningful.

Vintage Curves — Cumulative Default Rate by Months on Book
Different origination cohorts reaching different maturity levels

This chart shows why recency is dangerous for target variable construction. The Q3 2023 vintage shows only 3% default rate at 6 months — not because it's a better vintage, but because defaults haven't happened yet. The mature Q1 2022 vintage curves to 14%. Including immature vintages in your training data as if their current 3% is their final rate will cause your model to be systematically over-optimistic.

The rule of thumb: Only include loan cohorts that have reached at least 12 months on book (ideally 18 months) in your training dataset. Apply a performance window cutoff date and exclude all loans that haven't had enough observation time to manifest their default behaviour.

05 — MetricsChoosing the Right Evaluation Metric

EDA on the target variable should directly inform which metric you optimize for. Different banking use cases prioritize different error types.

Metric Selection Guide for Credit Risk Models
Driven by class imbalance level and business cost asymmetry
MetricWhat It MeasuresUse WhenAvoid When
AUC-ROC Ranking quality — ability to rank defaulters above non-defaulters Standard scorecard development, Gini coefficient reporting Threshold optimization or severe imbalance (>20:1)
KS Statistic Max separation between default and non-default score distributions Traditional scorecard validation; SBV model validation reports When score distributions overlap heavily
Precision-Recall AUC Precision vs. recall trade-off at every threshold Severe imbalance (>15:1); fraud detection adjacent use cases When ranking quality is more important than threshold performance
F1 Score Harmonic mean of precision and recall When you need a single-number threshold performance metric When FP and FN have very different business costs
Expected Loss PD × LGD × EAD — business cost of misclassification Final model approval; comparing against champion model Early development stages where LGD isn't modeled yet

Why Gini (= 2×AUC – 1) is standard in Vietnamese banking: SBV model validation teams are accustomed to Gini as the primary discriminatory power metric. A Gini above 0.40 is generally considered acceptable for consumer credit; above 0.55 is good; above 0.65 is excellent. Document the Gini in your EDA output so validators have a baseline before modeling begins.

06 — ValidationTarget Variable Validation Checklist

Before any modeling begins, every credit risk analyst should be able to confirm the following about their target variable. These are not optional — they are the minimum due diligence required for a defensible model.

is_default EDA Checklist

The fairness audit item: Regulatory frameworks increasingly require that credit models do not disproportionately deny credit to protected groups. Even if demographic features aren't in your model, proxy variables (postcode, employment type) can introduce disparate impact. EDA on the target variable should include a fairness check where possible — not just model performance metrics.

Understanding the target variable completely — its definition, its distribution, its temporal structure, and its variation across segments — is the foundation on which every subsequent modeling decision rests. No amount of feature engineering can compensate for a poorly understood or incorrectly defined target.
