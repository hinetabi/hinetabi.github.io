Outlier Detection —
When Extremes Tell the Truth

Le Thanh Binh · Data Scientist, Cake by VPBank · April 2026
Outliers Data Quality Risk Signals

A borrower with an annual income of 5 billion VND in a dataset where everyone else earns under 200 million. A loan amount of 0 VND. A DTI ratio of 4.7 when most values cluster between 0.1 and 0.9. Are these errors? Are they real? Should you remove them?

Outlier handling is where many data scientists make one of two opposite mistakes: they either remove too aggressively, throwing away the most informative records in the dataset, or they do nothing, letting extreme values distort every model they train. This part shows you how to think about outliers in banking — what they mean before you decide what to do with them.

01 — DefinitionWhat Are Outliers, Really?

An outlier is an observation that is significantly different from the majority of the data. The key word is "significantly" — which is where judgment comes in. Statistical tests give you candidates; domain knowledge tells you whether they're problems.

In a loan dataset, outliers appear in every numeric feature: loan amounts at the extremes, income figures that dwarf all others, debt-to-income ratios above 1.0 (technically impossible but present in real data due to measurement timing), or late payment counts far above the norm.

Why this matters for banking models: Most ML algorithms (linear regression, logistic regression, gradient boosting) are sensitive to extreme values. An outlier doesn't just affect the prediction for that one record — it shifts the weights learned from all other records. A single income value of 50 billion VND can move a regression coefficient dramatically.

02 — ClassificationThree Types of Outliers in Banking Data

Before deciding how to handle an outlier, classify it. Each type has a different correct response.

Outlier Classification Framework
Three distinct types requiring different responses

The distinction matters enormously. An erroneous outlier should be corrected or removed — it's noise. A legitimate outlier should almost never be removed — it represents a real borrower type your model needs to handle. An ambiguous outlier requires investigation: cross-reference another column, check the source system, or contact the data owner.

03 — MethodsThree Detection Approaches

There is no single correct method. Each technique has different assumptions and catches different kinds of extremes. Good practice uses at least two.

IQR Rule (Interquartile Range)

The most common approach in banking. Compute Q1 (25th percentile) and Q3 (75th percentile). The IQR is Q3 – Q1. Any value below Q1 – 1.5 × IQR or above Q3 + 1.5 × IQR is flagged. This is robust because it ignores the extreme values themselves when defining the boundaries.

IQR on loan_amount: If Q1 = 50M VND and Q3 = 300M VND, then IQR = 250M. Lower fence = 50M – 375M = –325M (no lower outliers). Upper fence = 300M + 375M = 675M VND. Any loan above 675M is an IQR outlier — worth examining, though not necessarily erroneous.

Z-Score (Standard Deviations from Mean)

Compute the mean (μ) and standard deviation (σ) of a feature. A Z-score of (x – μ) / σ measures how many standard deviations a value is from the mean. Values with |Z| > 3 are typically flagged. Limitation: Z-scores are themselves distorted by the outliers you're trying to detect. Use with caution on skewed distributions — and most banking variables (income, loan_amount) are heavily right-skewed.

Visual Inspection — Boxplots

Before any calculation, plot the data. Boxplots directly display the IQR fence and show individual outlier dots. They tell you not just that outliers exist, but how many there are, whether they're one-sided (right-skew) or symmetric, and how extreme they are relative to the bulk of the distribution.

05 — DecisionWhat to Do With Outliers

After identifying and classifying outliers, you have four options. Which one is right depends on the outlier type and your modeling objective.

Outlier Treatment Decision Table
Matched to outlier type and business context
TreatmentWhen to UseExample in BankingRisk
Remove Clearly erroneous; data entry mistake with no way to recover correct value income = 0 or age = 2 — impossible values Lose real data if misclassified
Cap (Winsorize) Legitimate but extreme; real customers you want to include, but value is too influential Cap loan_amount at 99th percentile (650M) — large loans still included but capped Distorts prediction for extreme-but-real cases
Transform Right-skewed distribution; log transform compresses the tail without removing data log(annual_income) — brings 5B VND CEO income closer to 500M median Coefficients harder to interpret in raw units
Keep as-is Outlier is informative; model needs to learn its behavior num_late_payments = 18 — this person is very high risk; removing them would create a blind spot Instability in linear models; tree models handle it fine
Flag & Segment Outlier represents a fundamentally different customer type needing separate treatment Create is_large_loan = True indicator; build separate model or apply separate policy rule Increases model complexity; needs more data per segment

The winsorization threshold question: Should you cap at the 95th, 99th, or 99.9th percentile? There is no universal answer. In consumer credit, 99th is common. In SME or corporate lending where variability is higher, 99.5th may be more appropriate. Always document your choice in your Model Development Document (MDD) so auditors and validators can review the justification.

06 — The ParadoxOutliers in Banking Are Often the Point

Here is the uncomfortable truth about outlier handling in credit risk: the "outliers" are often exactly the borrowers you need to understand most.

Consider num_late_payments. A borrower with 18 late payments is a massive outlier — most borrowers have 0 or 1. But this borrower is also, almost certainly, a defaulter. If you remove them as an outlier, you're removing your highest-risk cases from the training data. Your model will then be systematically blind to severe credit deterioration patterns.

late_payments vs. is_default — Why Outliers Must Stay
Removing outliers on the left removes the highest-default-rate segment

Borrowers with 15+ late payments default at 88%. These are unambiguously the most important observations for a credit risk model to learn from. The IQR fence might flag them as outliers — but removing them would be catastrophic for model quality and, ultimately, for the bank's ability to price risk correctly.

The right approach for late_payments: Keep all values as-is for model training. Consider log-transforming for linear models to reduce leverage. Create a binary flag is_repeat_defaulter if counts exceed a business threshold. Never remove — these records are signal, not noise.

Regulatory note: Under SBV Circular 11 and Basel model validation standards, your outlier treatment methodology must be documented and justified. "We removed outliers" is not acceptable documentation. The MDD must specify: which features, which thresholds, which method, and why — and must demonstrate that removal did not systematically exclude a protected or minority borrower group.

Outlier detection is ultimately a question of domain knowledge applied to data. No algorithm can tell you whether a 5-billion-VND income is real or erroneous — only the business context can. EDA is the bridge between the raw numbers and that judgment.
