Univariate Analysis — One Feature at a Time

Le Thanh Binh · Data Scientist, Cake by VPBank · April 2026

EDADistributionsBanking

Section 01What Is It & Why Do We Need It?

What is univariate analysis?

Univariate analysis examines one variable at a time — its shape, range, center, spread, and whether it behaves as expected given domain knowledge. Before asking how features relate to each other or to default, you must confirm each one makes sense on its own.

Why is it essential in banking?

Single-variable problems in banking data are far more common and consequential than in other domains. Each type of problem has a distinct signature:

Distribution shape misleads the model. annual_income is right-skewed — most borrowers earn 10–30M VND/month, a few earn 500M+. If you feed income directly into a linear model, the handful of wealthy outliers dominate the regression coefficients and degrade accuracy for the bulk of the population. You cannot know this without looking at the distribution first.

Impossible values reveal data quality failures. A customer_age of 5 or 200, a debt_to_income of 15 (meaning monthly debt is 15x income), a credit_score of 0 — these only surface when you inspect individual distributions. A model will train on them without complaint.

Bimodal distributions reveal hidden populations. If debt_to_income shows two distinct peaks — one around 0.2 (financially healthy) and one around 0.6 (financially stressed) — the dataset likely contains two fundamentally different customer segments that may behave very differently in default models. Treating them as one population produces a model that performs poorly for both.

Categorical imbalance creates blind spots. If only 10% of borrowers are Freelance, the model has very few examples from which to learn their behavior. If Freelancers also happen to default at higher rates, this is the segment most likely to be mis-scored — and the one the business most needs the model to get right.

Section 02Numerical Features — Credit Score

The credit score distribution is the first sanity check for the entire dataset. It should be approximately bell-shaped, centered somewhere between 550–700 for a typical consumer lending portfolio. A flat distribution, a spike at exactly 300, or a cliff at 850 would each signal a data pipeline problem.

Credit Score Distribution
Histogram · Near-normal, slight left skew · Mean ≈ 635 · Missing records excluded · n ≈ 55,000

What to look for here: The distribution is approximately normal with a slight left skew — a long tail toward 300. The peak at 600–650 represents the core of the portfolio. The 3% tail below 400 are high-risk borrowers who should almost certainly have high interest rates or be declined. If the minimum credit score in your portfolio is 580, but you find records at 320, those are worth investigating individually — they may be mis-mapped bureau scores, test accounts, or a specific product segment with different underwriting rules.

Section 03Skewness — Annual Income

Annual Income Distribution (VND / year)
Heavy right skew · Mean ≫ median · Long tail of high earners pulls statistics upward

Why skewness matters for modeling: The mean (142M VND) is nearly double the median (78M VND) — pulled up by a small group of very high earners. If you use mean income in a linear model's feature, the coefficient will be calibrated to the rich tail, not the typical borrower. The fix is a log-transform before training, which compresses the right tail and makes the feature approximately normal. This is not optional for income data in Vietnam — it is standard practice.

Section 04Bimodal Distributions — Hidden Customer Segments

A bimodal distribution — two distinct humps — is not just a statistical curiosity. In banking, it almost always signals that the dataset contains two fundamentally different customer populations that have been mixed together. Training a single model on them produces a model that fits neither well.

Debt-to-Income Ratio Distribution
Bimodal distribution · Two customer populations: financially healthy vs. financially stressed

Modeling implication of bimodal DTI: A model trained on both populations together will learn a single decision boundary that performs mediocrely for both. The right response — discovered through EDA — is to either segment the population (separate models for low-DTI and high-DTI customers) or add a flag feature is_high_dti = DTI > 0.45 that explicitly encodes which population each customer belongs to.

Section 05Categorical Features — Employment Type

Employment Type Distribution
Countplot · Freelancers are underrepresented at 10% — model will have few examples to learn from

Freelancers at 10% is a concerning imbalance. If Freelancers default at 12.4% (more than twice the portfolio average of 6%), the model has very few training examples for its highest-risk segment. In practice, this means: validate model performance separately for Freelancers during testing; consider oversampling or separate calibration for this group; flag it in the MDD as a known limitation.

Section 06Reading Guide — What to Look For in Each Chart

Univariate Chart Interpretation Guide
For every feature — ask these questions when reading its distribution
Chart TypeWhat to look forBanking red flags
HistogramShape (normal, skewed, bimodal), peak location, tail lengthSpike at a single value (sentinel); flat distribution (encoding issue); values outside physically possible range
BoxplotMedian, IQR spread, whisker length, outlier dotsMedian near one extreme; very short box (near-constant); extreme outlier dots that need investigation
CountplotRelative frequency of each category, which categories are rareA category with <1% frequency (will not model well); unexpected category names (test data, data errors)
Summary statsMean vs. median gap, min/max plausibility, standard deviationMean ≫ median (skew); min/max outside valid domain; std = 0 (constant feature, no signal)

The sanity check principle: For every numerical feature, ask: what is the minimum and maximum value that makes business sense? customer_age: 18–85. credit_score: 300–850. debt_to_income: 0–2 (DTI >1 is technically possible but extremely unusual — investigate every row above 1.5). interest_rate: 6–50% for Vietnamese consumer lending. Values outside these ranges are data quality issues, not outliers to keep.
