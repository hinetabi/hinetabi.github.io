Building and Monitoring a Credit Score Model

Problem Formulation

Le Thanh Binh  ·  2026

Before writing a single line of code, the most important work in an ML project happens in a whiteboard session with business stakeholders. In credit scoring, the business question sounds deceptively simple: who should we lend to? Unpacking that question carefully is what separates a model that ships and holds up in production from one that looks great on a validation set and fails quietly in the real world.

Business Goal → Model Goal

Mapping the Business Problem to a Model Objective

The business goal in retail lending is to maximize portfolio profitability while keeping non-performing loan (NPL) rates within an acceptable range. Translating this to an ML task requires three concrete decisions:

1. What is the prediction target?

The target label is a binary indicator of default. The most common definition is DPD90+: did the borrower go 90+ days past due at any point within 12 months of disbursement? But the choice of DPD threshold is itself a business decision with real tradeoffs — use the interactive chart below to explore them.

The "bad" definition is a business decision, not a technical one. Align with the risk team and model governance before labeling a single row — changing this definition later forces a full retrain.

2. What is the ML task?

This is a binary classification problem. The model outputs a probability of default (PD) for each applicant, then mapped to a score band:

  • Score bands (internal): A (lowest risk) → E (highest risk), typically mapped to approve / refer / decline decisions
  • Score scale (external): 300–850 style, or 0–1000, calibrated to match the bank's historical NPL rates per band
  • Outputs used by: automated decisioning engines, underwriter workstations, and portfolio risk dashboards

3. What metrics matter?

Define both ML and business metrics upfront — one drives model development, the other drives the deployment decision:

  • Gini coefficient = 2 × AUC − 1 — measures rank-ordering power. Target: Gini > 0.40 for retail credit
  • KS statistic — maximum separation between good and bad score CDFs. Target: KS > 30%
  • AUC-ROC — threshold-independent discrimination. Gini and KS are generally preferred in credit risk
  • Business metric: NPL rate reduction at the same approval rate, or approval rate lift at the same NPL rate

Feasibility

Feasibility Checklist

Running a feasibility check before scoping the full pipeline is not skepticism — it is good engineering discipline. Work through each item below before committing to the full lifecycle. Click each item to mark it assessed.

✓
Labeled data available?

Need loans disbursed ≥ 12 months ago with known DPD90+ outcomes. Recent vintages are still "performing" — not because borrowers are good, but because not enough time has passed.

✓
Meaningful signal-to-noise ratio?

Quick check: point-biserial correlation between top candidate features and the default label. If the strongest feature is below 0.05, the data may not support a useful model.

✓
Baseline performance documented?

What NPL rate and approval rate does the current policy (bureau cutoff / manual underwriting) achieve? The ML model must credibly beat this — not just in AUC, but in an operational scenario.

✓
Features serviceable at inference time?

Inference happens when the applicant submits — often within 2 seconds. Any feature requiring overnight batch computation needs a feature store cache. A feature that adds 800ms latency may not be worth it.

✓
Cost asymmetry quantified?

The false negative cost (approving a defaulter = principal + collection costs) is typically 3–5× the false positive cost (rejecting a good borrower = foregone margin). This ratio determines the production decision threshold.

Reject a borrower who would have repaid

Output of This Stage

By the end of problem formulation, the team should have a written one-pager that serves as the source of truth for the entire project:

01

ML task definition — binary classification, DPD90+ label, 12-month performance window, population scope (product, channel, customer segment)

02

Performance targets — minimum Gini (e.g., 0.40), target approval rate, acceptable NPL rate per score band

03

Feasibility assessment — data availability, baseline benchmark, feature latency constraints, any open risks

04

Cost asymmetry framing — FN/FP cost ratio that will determine the production decision threshold and evaluation protocol

05

Stakeholder sign-off — risk team, product, legal/compliance, and engineering aligned before any data work begins
