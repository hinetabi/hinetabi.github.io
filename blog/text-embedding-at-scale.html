Develop & Deploy Text Embedding Models at Scale

Le Thanh Binh  ·  2026  ·  18 min read

Text embeddings are one of the most quietly powerful primitives in modern ML. They turn unstructured text into dense vectors that capture semantic meaning — enabling semantic search, RAG pipelines, deduplication, clustering, and recommendations. Getting an embedding model to work in a notebook is easy. Getting it to work reliably at scale — low latency, high throughput, domain-accurate — is a different challenge entirely.

This post is a practical guide covering the full lifecycle: choosing a model, fine-tuning for your domain, optimizing inference, serving at scale, and evaluating in production. It draws from experience building embedding-powered systems at Coc Coc (search relevance for 25M+ monthly users) and Cake by VPBank (document understanding pipelines).

Section 1

What Are Text Embeddings, and Why Do They Matter?

An embedding model maps a piece of text — a word, sentence, or document — to a fixed-size vector in a high-dimensional space. The key property: semantically similar texts end up near each other. "How do I reset my password?" and "I forgot my login credentials" will cluster together, even though they share no keywords.

This unlocks capabilities that keyword-based systems simply can't do:

  • Semantic search — retrieve documents by meaning, not just lexical overlap
  • RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) — ground LLM outputs in relevant context chunks
  • Deduplication — detect near-duplicate content across millions of records
  • Zero-shot classification — classify without labeled examples, using embedding similarity
  • Recommendation — find similar items based on textual descriptions

The scale problem. Encoding 10 million documents at 20ms/doc takes ~55 hours on a single CPU. Serving 1,000 QPS at 50ms p99 requires careful batching, hardware selection, and concurrency design. These are engineering problems, not just ML ones.

Benchmarking with MTEB

The Massive Text Embedding Benchmark (MTEB) is the standard leaderboard for embedding models. It covers 56 datasets across 8 task types: retrieval, clustering, classification, semantic similarity, and more. Before picking a model, check its MTEB score — but also run your own domain-specific eval, because general benchmarks often don't reflect performance on specialized vocabulary (medical, legal, fintech).

Tip: Dimensionality matters for storage and retrieval speed. A 1536-dim vector costs 2× more to store and index than a 768-dim one. Use Matryoshka Representation Learning (MRL) models like text-embedding-3-large to truncate dimensions without re-embedding — you can drop from 3072 to 256 dims with minimal quality loss.

Section 3

Fine-tuning for Your Domain

General-purpose models are trained on web-crawl data. If your documents contain domain-specific terminology — financial reports, medical records, legal contracts, Vietnamese colloquial text — a fine-tuned model will consistently outperform an off-the-shelf one on retrieval tasks.

The Training Objective: Contrastive Learning

Embedding models are typically fine-tuned with a contrastive loss. The key idea: given a query q, pull its positive passage p⁺ closer in vector space, and push negative passages p⁻ further away.

Data Preparation: The Critical Step

The quality of your training data matters more than the choice of architecture. A model trained on 10,000 high-quality (query, passage) pairs will outperform one trained on 100,000 noisy pairs. Here's the pipeline I use:

01
Mine positive pairs
Collect (query, relevant document) pairs from user logs (click data, search sessions). If logs aren't available, generate synthetic queries with an LLM given a passage as input.
02
Mine hard negatives
Use BM25 or a weak embedding model to retrieve top-k candidates for each query, then treat non-relevant ones as hard negatives. Easy negatives (random docs) are not enough — the model needs to learn fine-grained distinctions.
03
Filter with a cross-encoder
A cross-encoder reranker (e.g., cross-encoder/ms-marco-MiniLM-L-6-v2) can score (query, passage) pairs with high accuracy. Use it to remove false negatives — passages that are actually relevant but weren't labeled as such.
04
Train with in-batch negatives
With in-batch negatives, every other example in the batch serves as a negative. This makes large batches critical — use GradCache or gradient checkpointing to fit large batch sizes without OOM errors.

Section 4

Optimizing for Inference Speed

A 110M-parameter BERT-based model can process ~100 sentences/second on a single CPU core. To hit production throughput targets, you need a combination of hardware, batching, and model compression strategies.

Quantization with ONNX Runtime

Exporting a model to ONNX and applying INT8 quantization typically yields a 2–4× speedup with less than 1% quality degradation on most retrieval benchmarks. This is almost always the first optimization to reach for.

Python · Export to ONNX + INT8 Quantization
from optimum.onnxruntime import ORTModelForFeatureExtraction
from optimum.onnxruntime.configuration import AutoQuantizationConfig

# Export to ONNX
ort_model = ORTModelForFeatureExtraction.from_pretrained(
    "bge-m3-finetuned", export=True
)

# INT8 dynamic quantization (CPU-friendly)
qconfig = AutoQuantizationConfig.arm64(is_static=False, per_channel=False)
quantizer.quantize(
    save_dir="bge-m3-onnx-int8",
    quantization_config=qconfig,
)

Batching Strategy

The single biggest throughput lever for GPU inference is batch size. But dynamic traffic makes static batching wasteful — use a queue-based micro-batcher that accumulates requests over a short window (5–10ms) and fires them as a single batch.

Section 5

Serving at Scale

Once the model is optimized, the next challenge is packaging it for production serving with proper observability, scaling, and failure handling.

Containerizing with Triton Inference Server

NVIDIA Triton is purpose-built for serving ML models at high throughput. It supports ONNX Runtime natively, handles dynamic batching server-side, and exposes both HTTP and gRPC endpoints. For embedding models, it's one of the highest-performance options available.

Async Inference for Bulk Encoding

For offline batch encoding (indexing millions of documents), async I/O with a producer-consumer queue dramatically outperforms naive sequential calls. Use a asyncio producer to read documents, a worker pool to call the model, and a consumer to write vectors to the database.

Section 6

Vector Storage & Retrieval

Embedding vectors are useless without a storage backend that can find the nearest neighbors efficiently. At scale, you need an Approximate Nearest Neighbor (ANN) index — exact search over millions of 768-dim vectors is too slow.

HNSW vs. IVF-Flat: Choosing Your Index

HNSW (Hierarchical Navigable Small World) builds a graph structure that enables fast greedy search. It offers the best query latency at the cost of more RAM and slower build time. IVF-Flat clusters vectors into buckets (inverted file), which is faster to build and more memory-efficient but requires tuning the number of probes at query time. For most production use cases under 50M vectors, HNSW wins on query speed.

Section 7

Evaluating in Production

Offline MTEB scores tell you how a model ranks globally. They don't tell you whether it's working for your users. Production evaluation requires a different approach.

Offline Evaluation: NDCG@k

Build a labeled evaluation set: a set of queries with human-annotated relevant documents. Measure NDCG@10 (Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain) — it rewards systems that rank the most relevant results higher. Run this eval on every model checkpoint before deploying.

Online Evaluation: A/B Testing

Shadow the new embedding model alongside the current one, routing a percentage of live traffic to each. Measure the business metric that matters: click-through rate for search, context relevance for RAG (using an LLM-as-judge), or downstream task accuracy. A 2% NDCG improvement offline doesn't always translate to a win in production.

LLM-as-judge for RAG. For RAG evaluation, use an LLM to score (question, retrieved context, answer) triples on faithfulness and answer relevance. Libraries like RAGAS and TruLens automate this pipeline and give you a dashboard of retrieval quality metrics.

Monitoring Embedding Drift

If your document distribution changes over time (new products, new terminology, new user intents), the embedding space can drift — queries that previously retrieved relevant results start failing. Monitor the distribution of cosine similarities between queries and their top-1 retrieved documents. A significant drop in average similarity is a signal to re-index or retrain.

Section 8

Lessons Learned & Pitfalls

Here are the issues that bit me most often in practice:

!
Always L2-normalize your embeddings
Cosine similarity = dot product on unit-norm vectors. If you forget to normalize, your retrieval results can be dominated by vector magnitude rather than semantic similarity. Add model.encode(texts, normalize_embeddings=True) — always.
!
Prompt prefixes matter more than you think
Models like BGE and E5 are trained with instruction prefixes: "Represent this sentence for searching relevant passages: " for queries, and no prefix for passages. Omitting the prefix silently degrades retrieval quality by 5–15% on NDCG.
!
Stale indexes are a silent killer
After fine-tuning, you must re-encode all your documents — the vector space has shifted. Partial re-encoding (only new docs) creates inconsistent neighbors and degrades retrieval quality subtly. Always full re-index after a model update.
!
Token truncation is a retrieval trap
BERT-based models have a 512-token limit. Long documents are truncated silently, and important information at the end is lost. For long-document retrieval, chunk documents into overlapping 256–512 token windows and embed each chunk separately.
Section 9

Conclusion

Text embedding at scale is a full-stack problem. The ML is only one layer — choosing the right model and fine-tuning it for your domain. The rest is systems engineering: quantization and batching for throughput, Triton or a similar server for deployment, a vector DB with the right index for retrieval, and a robust evaluation loop to catch regressions before they reach users.

The ecosystem is moving fast. Matryoshka embeddings are making dimension reduction practical. Sparse-dense hybrid retrieval (combining BM25 and dense embeddings) is closing the gap with reranking pipelines. And multimodal embeddings — text + image in a shared space — are opening entirely new use cases.

If I had to summarize the three things that move the needle most in practice: mine hard negatives for fine-tuning, always normalize embeddings, and don't skip the production evaluation loop. The rest is details you'll work out as you go.
