Building and Monitoring a Credit Score Model

Business Impact Estimation

Le Thanh Binh  ·  2026

A model that improves Gini by 5 points over the baseline is a meaningful technical achievement. Whether it is worth deploying is a different question — one that requires translating model performance into the language the business cares about: NPL rate, approval rate, revenue impact, and expected credit losses. This translation is the purpose of business impact estimation, and it is what converts a model evaluation into a deployment decision.

Lift and Threshold

Lift Analysis and Decision Threshold Selection

Cumulative gains and lift curves

  • A cumulative gains curve plots the percentage of defaults captured as a function of the percentage of applicants rejected, ordered by model score from highest risk to lowest.
  • If the model perfectly rank-orders applicants, rejecting the top 10% by risk would capture a disproportionate share of future defaults — this is the model's lift over random selection.
  • In practice: if your current policy rejects 15% of applicants and captures 30% of defaults, but the new model at the same 15% rejection rate captures 55% of defaults, you have the same approval rate with dramatically fewer defaults.

Precision-recall and threshold selection

  • The credit score cutoff (the score below which applicants are declined) determines the operating point on the precision-recall curve.
  • Moving the cutoff up increases approval rate and revenue but accepts higher NPL; moving it down improves credit quality but reduces volume.
  • The right cutoff is a business policy decision, not a statistical one — involve the risk committee, finance (NPL budget owners), and sales (approval volume targets).
  • Present the full precision-recall curve with annotations showing approval rate and NPL at each threshold, letting business pick the operating point.

Cost-Benefit

Cost-Benefit Framing on the Confusion Matrix

In credit scoring, every cell in the confusion matrix has a monetary value:

Correctly declined bad borrower
Value: +5M VND
(LGD avoided)
False Positive
Incorrectly declined good borrower
Cost: −1.2M VND
(lost margin)
False Negative
Incorrectly approved bad borrower
Cost: −5M VND
(credit loss)
True Negative
Correctly approved good borrower
Value: +1.2M VND
(interest earned)

With these values, the expected profit at any score threshold is: (TP × LGD_savings) + (TN × margin) − (FP × foregone_margin) − (FN × credit_loss). This formula can be evaluated across the full range of thresholds to find the profit-maximizing cutoff, or to show the business how much expected value each additional percentage point of approval rate costs in NPL exposure.

LGD and margin estimates do not need to be exact for this analysis to be useful. Even rough order-of-magnitude values produce a curve that gives stakeholders a concrete basis for the threshold discussion. The goal is to replace "we improved Gini by 0.05" with "at our current approval rate, the model is projected to reduce credit losses by X billion VND annually."

Simulation

Pre-Launch Retrospective Simulation

When a live A/B test is not feasible before deployment — which is common in banking because you cannot randomly assign some applicants to a "bad model" condition — retrospective simulation on held-out historical data is the next best approach.

The simulation setup

  1. 1
    Take the test set: most recent 6 months of origination data, held out from training
  2. 2
    Apply the new model's decisions as if it had run in production during that period: approve above cutoff, decline below
  3. 3
    Simulate portfolio outcomes under the new model: NPL rate, approval rate, disbursed volume, interest income
  4. 4
    Compare against baseline: what actually happened under the existing policy

What the simulation answers

  • NPL rate reduction: how many defaults would have been avoided with the new model
  • Approval rate change: how many good borrowers would have been approved or declined differently
  • Net revenue impact: interest income gained or lost due to the changed approval pattern

Limitations to communicate

  • Censored population problem: the simulation operates on the approved population only; loans declined under the existing policy are not in the test set, so the simulation cannot assess what would have happened if the model had approved some previously rejected applicants.
  • Be explicit about this limitation when presenting to stakeholders — it prevents overconfidence in the simulation's projections and ensures decision-makers understand the scope of the analysis.
Presentation

Presenting the Business Case

The output of this stage is a business case document, not just a model performance report. It must contain:

  • 1
    Lift curve at the proposed cutoff vs. the current policy, showing how much you improve default capture at a given rejection rate
  • 2
    Projected NPL reduction expressed in absolute expected loss (VND, not just percentage points), with clear link to balance sheet impact
  • 3
    Approval rate impact: how many additional applicants will be approved, and revenue implications
  • 4
    Net expected value of deployment: avoided losses minus any foregone revenue from stricter selection — the bottom-line business case

This document is what business leadership, risk management, and often the board's risk committee will use to approve the model for production deployment.
